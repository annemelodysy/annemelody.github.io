Tokyo was never part of the plan. A few months before the trip, we were set on traveling to Mexico to explore ancient pyramids and lounge on the gorgeous white sand beaches. The timing was right too—DÃ­a de Los Muertos was around the corner and we were excited to witness the colorful parades. Then we got a message from our friends back home.

One of the coolest couples we know were about to say their vows so we had a decision to make. "Should we go?" I asked Jeremie. It took us less than five minutes to get the answer. We were both yearning to go home realizing only when faced with an opportunity.

"We can stop over in Japan on our way back," An irresistible proposal. I've always been curious about Japan—its culture, food, streets, and landscapes. Exposed by animated films portraying worlds so magical, so different from mine, I grew up hoping I could one day see it for myself.

"You'll be fine. Everything in Japan is good."

—A recurring reaction among friends who've been to Japan when we asked for recommendations. I took this advice literally but found out too late that it wasn't accurate. Note to self: people exaggerate. While the majority of food were above average (some convenience stores carried meals that were surpisingly good), a handful of restaurants were mediocre.

It also turns out that getting reservations several days ahead is necessary if you want to dine at a specific restaurant. In San Francisco, most restaurants accept walk-ins even if they're full. The cost is usually a few hours of wait. The longest we were told to wait was for FOUR hours. We ended up eating somewhere else, but hey, we had a choice.

In Tokyo, you hardly see lines. We foolishly took this as good news for us at first. It wasn't until we spoke to the host/hostess that we found out that there were no lines because they turned people away if you had no reservations.

I hardly took pictures of food because it felt awkward to bring out my camera while everyone was enjoying their meals. Tokyo restaurants (at least the ones we visited) were so quiet that moving around with a relatively large camera was easily noticeable.

The only photos I managed to take were in Akomeya tucked somewhere in the Ginza neighborhood. It's a grocery store that comes with a tiny, sort-of-hidden restaurant at the back. The menu had only one set meal using seasonal ingredients. The whole set was good (though some were unfamiliar), but my favorite was the rice. Every bite was heaven. I've never had rice that delicious!

In one of the alleys in Shinjuku, we happened upon a hole-in-the-wall. It had an unassuming exterior, worn-out and cluttered. A middle-aged man sat on the tiniest chair lighting a fire on what looked like a portable grill. We could smell grilled food from outside but was unsure about going in because there were no English signs.

A man emerged from the sliding doorway and gestured for us to come in. Without a single word, he led us to a very narrow aisle crammed with humans. A dense layer of smoke enveloped the room mixing the scent of both grilled food and lit cigarettes.

We squeezed our way to the back of the room where a table was ready for us.

"Beer?" The man raised his arm to gesture drinking from a glass.

"Water, please."

"No water. Beer, coke?"

Jeremie and I looked at each other.

"2 Cokes, please."

He disappeared in the crowd. Minutes later he came back with our drinks and asked us if we wanted some pork stomach.

"You mean pork belly?" I wondered if there was simply a translation barrier.

"Yes. Pork stomach?"

"Pork belly?" I asked again, thinking he might not have heard me because of the loud conversations in the room.

One of the diners intervened and tried to describe what I mean.

"Yes...pork stomach."

I looked at Jeremie, then at the man. "Do you mean pork bel..."

"Yes, we'll have that." Jeremie cut me off. He was getting hangry. The man placed a plate on our table before disappearing into the crowd once again.

It was definitely not pork belly.

Here's what I've learned:

  1. Translations are tricky. But if you want to be adventurous, just say yes.
  2. Make reservations ahead of time for special restaurants that you really want to try.
  3. But keep your plans loose. You'll find surprises in unexpected places.

A singular unit

Rush hour was a beast. The only time I got my camera out was in the smaller stations where there were less people. Since I don't have photos of what it's like, I'll just try to describe it.

Train stations in Tokyo feel like its own unique world, becoming more apparent during rush hours. Once you step foot in one, you are no longer an individual nor a human being. You lose your identity. You become nothing more than a set of limbs—a part of a whole, moving in a direction—forward, never back. There are rules to follow and going beyond them means you risk ending up to where you started or worse, you get stuck in a loop.

It's the same rule when you're on the highway, match your speed with the rest or get out of the way. Adjust your pace so that it synchronizes with the rest of the limbs. There are no conversations; the only sound you hear comes from the trains and the synchronized footsteps.

The city was unlike anywhere we've been with its infinite alleys tucked between compact buildings. We devoured every scene in front of us, stopping only to take a breath or to find something to eat. During the day, we walked among the busy working class, watched them pass us, wondering where they were heading.

Organized Chaos

At night, neon lights dominated the urban landscape, illuminating the thousands of people rushing in every direction. My heart raced and my gut sank as I watched the world around me. It wasn't excitement that I felt but anxiety triggered by the inescapable chaos of this city.

It was a different kind of chaos, nothing like the other big cities we've been. There was enough space to move around despite the crowds and people were respectful of your space.

Organized chaos. These are words we often hear people describe Tokyo.
